InnStar Brand
System.

Six practices under one holding company, shaped over ten years into a single editorial logic. The long argument that a luxury ensemble in Dhaka could be read, from the outside, as one clear voice in six registers.

I. The Premise
One voice, six registers.

In 2016, InnStar owned three operating practices and two more were drafted on a whiteboard. Each had its own logo, its own colour, its own brochure, its own tone. Read together they read as five companies, not one. Read alone, each practice read smaller than it was.

The argument, made first on paper and then slowly made in the work, was that a holding company is not the sum of its practices. It is a position. The position holds, and every practice earns the right to speak from within it. The practices could differ in register. They could not differ in conviction.

II. Architecture
Branded house, quietly.

The architecture arrived at is a branded-house system. InnStar is the mark of origin. Each practice carries a practice-level mark, set in the same typographic grammar, in the same weight and width, with a single-letter prefix.

A printed document, a sign, a uniform, a business card — the reader recognises it as an InnStar document within a quarter-second, and recognises the practice within a second more. The recognition is carried by the system, not by the logo. This is the position we defended, quietly, for ten years.

III. The Six Practices
Distinct work, single register.

Each practice is described in a single editorial paragraph on the holding company website, in identical structure and length. The discipline of the equal paragraph was, more than any logo decision, what made the system hold.

IV. Typography
Two families, one grid.

The editorial system is carried by two families: a humanist serif for display and narrative (the practice text on a sign, the headline on a brochure, the opening paragraph of a proposal) and a restrained grotesque for operations and governance (plans, addresses, legal, wayfinding).

The rule is not rigid. The serif occasionally takes a signage job. The sans occasionally takes a display. What does not move is the grid: the twelve-column editorial grid, the 24pt baseline, and the margin:copy ratio drawn for the holding company brochure in 2017 and enforced on every document printed since.

“The recognition is carried by the system, not by the logo.”
V. Tone of Voice
Restraint as a discipline.

The tone guide is four pages. It fits on four pages because most of it is a list of what we do not do. We do not use superlatives. We do not write in the first-person plural unless legally necessary. We do not use the words solutions, premium, exclusive, world-class, passionate, unique, journey, partnership, or excellence. We do not end sentences with exclamation marks.

The positive guidance is shorter still. Write short sentences. Name what is named. Decline the sales line. Trust the reader.

VI. Training & Governance
The hardest surface.

A brand system lives or fails at the point of the thousandth artefact: the internal memo, the vendor email, the printed sign at the back of a service corridor. The hardest surface, in other words, is not the one the designer makes. It is the one the designer is not in the room for.

From 2019 onward, a quarterly brand clinic was scheduled for every function in the holding company. Finance, HR, Facilities, Marketing, Legal. Thirty minutes of reading together. Thirty minutes of rewriting. The vocabulary of luxury made practical. Curriculum: Elements of Luxury, The Editorial Memo, What We Don’t Say.

VII. Record
Ten years of the same conviction.

Signage and wayfinding across 19 residential and commercial projects. Three properties to LEED certification. Campaigns run in partnership with Telenor Group and Meta. Market entry for Jinpeng EV in 2025. A luxury residential experience center directed from brief to opening. An Outrenoir award in 2023.

None of this was the position. The position was the discipline that produced it, and the discipline that will produce whatever is next.

VIII. Notes in Closing
What the work is for.

A brand system is not a guidelines document. It is a long argument, made over years, that a business can behave with editorial consistency while its practices continue to differ in the world. The argument has to be renewed, in meeting rooms and on copy decks, every quarter, or it falls.

The underlying conviction remains intact. The luxury register will hold if it is asked to. It has to be asked to, in practical sentences, in decisions made in the room, for ten years, without drama.
