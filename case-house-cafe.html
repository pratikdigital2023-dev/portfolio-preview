Identity · Interior · Menu · Music Direction · 2022–24

House Café.

A neighbourhood café in Dhaka reimagined as a small listening room. Identity, menu architecture, interior direction, and a commissioned music programme drawn from the jazz-era city that Dhaka once was.

Client
House Café · Independent operator
Role
Brand Experience Director, full scope
Scope
Name, identity, interior brief, menu system, music direction, uniform, service script
Typology
Neighbourhood café · 32 covers · Open kitchen
Duration
2022 → 2024 (in service)
Programme
Coffee · All-day kitchen · Vinyl listening evenings
I. The Brief
Not another café.

The operator had already owned two coffee businesses in Dhaka. Both had failed gracefully, for the same reason. They were cafés. Nothing more. The city is full of cafés and the marginal one does not last.

The brief we agreed on, across a long afternoon and a notebook, was that the third attempt would not be a café that also played music. It would be a room organised around listening, which happened to serve coffee. The distinction looks small on paper. It decides everything that follows.

II. The Name
A house, not a shop.

House is the jazz word. A house band. A house orchestra. A house set. It is also the Dhaka domestic register: the door a neighbour walks through without knocking. Café is the category, listed plainly, in the same weight as the rest of the name, so the category cannot hide.

We resisted a more clever name, and a more poetic one, and a Bangla-first one. The most useful thing the name could do was get out of the way of the room.

III. Identity
A record label, not a café brand.

The identity system was built as if House Café were a small independent record label. The wordmark sits inside a square, at the top-left quadrant, in the way a Blue Note or ECM sleeve sets its logo. Everything printed, menus, coasters, napkin rings, the vinyl sleeve for the house compilation, reads as a catalogue.

The typographic pairing is Cormorant Garamond for display and Univers 47 for detail. The palette is bone, cream, and a single soft black. There is no accent colour. The accent is the music.

IV. The Menu
A side A and a side B.

The menu is structured as an LP. Side A is morning and coffee. Side B is afternoon and kitchen. There are two specials a week, written in by hand, and a footnote rather than a dessert list. The menu is one folded sheet of Hahnemühle, reprinted weekly, numbered in the corner like a pressing.

“The menu is one folded sheet, reprinted weekly, numbered in the corner like a pressing.”

Every dish is named for a recording. The Monk, the Ella, the Ravi. Guests who know the reference know it. Guests who don’t read an ordinary menu and order an ordinary breakfast.

V. Music Direction
Jazz-era Dhaka.

Most of the difficult thinking went into the music. The city had a brief, astonishing jazz scene in the late 1960s: radio broadcasts out of what was then called Dhaka Radio, visiting orchestras, a small club circuit that has since been paved over. We commissioned a researcher to sit in the national archive for six weeks and return with every recording, photograph, and programme note from 1965 to 1971 she could legitimately copy.

What came back became the basis for the house programme. Monday is Blue Note. Tuesday is the archive. Wednesday is new pressings. Thursday is listening night, a single record played end to end, lights low, no conversation louder than the speaker. The room, we found, will hold that rule once asked.

VI. Interior
A listening room.

The interior team was briefed against every contemporary café cliché. No exposed Edison bulbs. No neon. No moss walls. No quote on the wall. The reference set was a Kamakura tea house, a small Japanese jazz kissaten, and the small front rooms of Dhaka houses built between 1960 and 1975.

Speakers (a pair of vintage Tannoys, rebuilt by a retired sound engineer in Mirpur) sit at the two ends of the room. Tables are arranged so no seat is more than three metres from a speaker. The kitchen is open. The pass is kept deliberately low, so conversation from the kitchen does not rise above the record.

VII. Notes in Closing
What the work is for.

House Café has been in service since the autumn of 2024. Thursday listening night is booked two weeks out. The archive programme has been picked up for a short residency at a small venue in Banani. The third attempt has not failed.

The conviction was that a room, disciplined, can ask something of the people in it, and they will meet the ask. This remains the conviction.
