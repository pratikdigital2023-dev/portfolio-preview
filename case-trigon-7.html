Trigon 7
at Dewans.

A thirty-storey diagrid tower on Gulshan Avenue. Paris register, Dhaka address. Positioning, naming logic, and the narrative architecture for one of the city’s most visible luxury residential projects.

Client
Dewan Group · Real Estate Division
Role
Brand Experience Director · AGM, InnStar
Scope
Naming, positioning, editorial voice, launch collateral, sales gallery direction
Typology
Thirty storeys · Diagrid structural expression · Luxury residential
Duration

Gulshan Avenue, Dhaka
I. The Brief
What the tower was asked to be.

Dewan Group came with a structural ambition and a plot on the most photographed avenue in the city. Thirty storeys. A diagrid exoskeleton. Apartments at a price register the Bangladesh market had not seen published before.

The brief was not a logo. The brief was a register. Who does this building speak to, and in what voice, and in which language, and alongside which peers. The answer had to hold up next to Haussmann, not next to the towers immediately beside it.

II. Positioning
Paris register, Dhaka address.

The positioning line we arrived at was not an advertising line. It was a brief to ourselves. Everything we made afterward, the name, the brochure, the sales gallery, the show flat, the launch film, had to hold that register or be cut.

Luxury in Dhaka residential had largely meant surface. Marble, gold, uplighters. We proposed the opposite discipline. Quiet materials. Generous proportion. Editorial restraint in the collateral. A building that knew what it was and did not need to announce it twice.

“A building that knew what it was and did not need to announce it twice.”
III. The Name
Trigon, not Trident.

The diagrid is made of triangles. The most obvious naming route was to reference the geometry directly. We argued away from that, toward a name that described what the geometry did rather than what it looked like.

Trigon — Greek, three-cornered, first recorded in English architecture in the 1560s. 7 — a prime, the height register in Gulshan (the building sits at the 7-road intersection), and, quietly, the seven corners of the plot itself. at Dewans — the preposition placed the building in a neighbourhood of Dewan-built addresses, rather than in a catalogue of towers.

IV. Collateral
The brochure as a book.

The launch brochure was specified as a 120-page hardcover, printed letterpress on Gmund Cotton, trimmed short of the jacket. The decision was made at the outset that buyers would not be asked to read a sales document. They would be asked to read a book about a building.

Floorplans were set as architectural drawings, not as sales pages. Specifications were listed once, in a colophon, not repeated across every spread. The photography brief specified raking morning light and no people, in the tradition of Bernd and Hilla Becher.

V. The Sales Gallery
A room, not a showroom.

The sales gallery occupies a single room on the ground floor of a Dewan property two blocks from the site. The brief to the interior team was that a prospective buyer should be able to sit down, close a catalogue, and look at nothing but the diagrid model under directional light for as long as they wished to.

There is one screen in the room. There is no music. The conversation is carried by a single advisor and a single bound book.

VI. Notes in Closing
What the work is for.

Trigon 7 at Dewans has not yet topped out. The launch campaign is scheduled for the spring following structural completion. This case study will be amended as photography becomes available and as the register holds, or does not hold, in the wild.

The underlying conviction remains intact. Quiet is a position. Restraint is a strategy. A building tells you what it is before the brochure does, and the brochure’s only job is not to contradict it.
